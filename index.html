State of Maine's Forests · 2026

The most forested state in America, measured.

Maine's 17.4 million acres of forestland are the state's single most important climate asset — removing more carbon dioxide than Maine emits from all sources combined, while anchoring an $8.3 billion economy. Every figure on this dashboard is measured in the field or officially reported. Nothing is modeled.

Aligned with the Maine Climate Council Scientific & Technical Subcommittee forest indicators (Forest Carbon & Productivity; Forest Health & Diversity, 2026).

01 — Climate asset

Climate & Carbon

Maine's forests are the centerpiece of Maine Won't Wait, the state climate plan. Natural and working lands already offset the large majority of Maine's emissions, putting the state among the closest in the nation to its 2045 carbon-neutrality target.

02 — Forest carbon

Forest Carbon & Productivity

Maine forests store roughly 1.59 billion metric tons of carbon and keep growing far more wood than is harvested. They are one of the few places in the country whose lands absorb more carbon than the entire state emits.

🌲

What the data tell us. Carbon storage is up 4.6% since 2003, harvest remains highly sustainable (grow 72% more than is cut), and tree mortality is declining. Forests are Maine's number-one climate asset — every forested acre lost permanently reduces the state's capacity to remove carbon.

03 — Resilience

Forest Health & Diversity

Maine's forests are getting denser and, in places, more stressed. Diversity is holding steady, wildfire risk stays very low, but a set of invasive pests is spreading fast.

Invasive pests are spreading

Number of Maine counties (of 16) affected by each major invasive pest.

Climate warming is expected to increase pest range and overwintering survival. Species and structural diversity remain stable, providing a solid ecological foundation for resilience.

Forest maturity & habitat

FIA field measurements of stand age, size class, and standing dead wood, 2024. Compiled via the CRSF PERSEUS data pipeline.

🌲

What the data tell us. Stand density is approaching concerning levels — strategic thinning would improve resilience and produce wood products. Beech leaf disease is now statewide; emerald ash borer and hemlock woolly adelgid keep expanding. Wildfire risk is very low, but drought risk is rising under climate change.

The working forest

About 8.5 million acres are third-party certified. Maine's forests sustain a $8.3 billion economy while still growing far more wood than is harvested.

04 — Working forest

Economy & Products

Maine's forest products sector contributes $8.3 billion a year and supports nearly 30,000 jobs — one of every 29 dollars of state GDP. It leads the nation in forest-sector share of exports.

🌲

What the data tell us. The sustainable harvest that keeps growth ahead of cutting is the same harvest that sustains mills, jobs, and rural communities. Maine's challenge is measuring the trade-offs — carbon, timber, habitat — not choosing one over the others.

Kept intact

Roughly 4.1 million acres — about a fifth of Maine's forest — are conserved, keeping the north woods whole, working, and open to the public.

05 — The land

Land & Conservation

Nearly 88% of Maine is forested. About 4.1 million acres — roughly 22% of the forest — are conserved through ownership or easement, and 85% of easement forestland stays in active working-forest management.

🌲

What the data tell us. Forest cover is stable at 87.8% — the highest of any state — but has slipped 1.4 points since 2003, driven by development in southern Maine. Conservation has accelerated sharply since the 1990s, keeping most of the north woods intact and working.

07 — How we know this

Measured, not modeled

Every number on this dashboard comes from field measurement or official reporting. For future projections and scenario modeling, see the multi-model PERSEUS Forest Intelligence platform.

Forest inventory

USDA Forest Inventory & Analysis (FIA) — 3,500+ permanent field plots. The county map's carbon, age, old-forest, diversity, ownership, and harvest layers are design-based FIA estimates. Vintage: 1999–2003 baseline through 2024.

Forest Service & state

Maine Forest Service annual pest surveys, wood-processor reports, and the biennial State of the Forest.

Carbon accounting

Maine State Carbon Budget v2 and Hayes et al. (2024) for emissions offset. Vintage: 2017–2021.

Satellite & land cover

NLCD land cover and Hansen Global Forest Change for forest-loss mapping.

Economics

UMaine FIDACS and the Maine Forest Products Council. Vintage: 2023.

Conservation & boundaries

Maine DACF conserved-lands GIS and US Census TIGER county boundaries.

What this dashboard is not

This is an observational record of Maine's forests, released annually as the citable data product for the State of Maine's Forests. It carries no model projections, no scenarios, and no forecasts. For climate-scenario carbon projections and management modeling, that work lives in the CRSF carbon-budget and PERSEUS platforms.
